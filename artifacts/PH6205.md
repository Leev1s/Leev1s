# Topic 4: Poisson Regression and Count Models

## Count Data and Model Choice

Count data are numerical discrete data taking non-negative integer values such as 0, 1, 2, and so on. Examples from the course include numbers of disease cases, bottles of beer drunk last week, insurance claims, and HPAI cases. Count data are not continuous, so ordinary linear regression is not appropriate. Count data are also not binary unless the only possible values are 0 and 1, so binary logistic regression is not appropriate for general count outcomes.

Use Poisson regression when the outcome is a count and the variance is roughly equal to the mean. If the variance is larger than the mean, the data show overdispersion; use quasi-Poisson or negative binomial regression. If there are more zeros than a plain Poisson model can explain, use a zero-inflated Poisson model. If the variance is smaller than the mean, this is underdispersion, and negative binomial regression is not the right fix because it is designed for overdispersed count data.

## Poisson Distribution

If a count variable follows a Poisson distribution, write

$$
Y \sim \mathrm{Pois}(\lambda).
$$

The probability of observing k is

$$
P(Y=k)=\frac{\lambda^k e^{-\lambda}}{k!},\qquad k=0,1,2,\ldots
$$

The parameter lambda is positive and does not need to be an integer. It is both the mean and the variance:

$$
E(Y)=\lambda,\qquad \mathrm{Var}(Y)=\lambda.
$$

If a random variable Y is multiplied by a constant a, then the mean becomes a times the original mean, and the variance becomes a squared times the original variance. Therefore, aY is generally not Poisson because the mean and variance will no longer be equal in the Poisson way.

## Poisson Regression With Offset

Poisson regression models the expected count. With count outcome Y, mean lambda, covariates X, and exposure T, the model is

$$
\log(\lambda)=\beta_0+\beta_1X_1+\cdots+\beta_pX_p+\log(T).
$$

Equivalently, it is a rate model:

$$
\log\left(\frac{\lambda}{T}\right)=\beta_0+\beta_1X_1+\cdots+\beta_pX_p.
$$

The log link is used because lambda must be positive, while the linear predictor can range from negative infinity to positive infinity. The offset is log(T), and its coefficient is fixed at 1. Use an offset when observations have different population sizes, exposure times, areas, or other exposure amounts. If the exposure is the same for all observations, no offset is needed.

The fitted mean is

$$
\lambda^{\mathrm{fit}}=e^{b_0}e^{b_1X_1}\cdots e^{b_pX_p}T.
$$

In R, a typical formula is

```r
glm(Y ~ X1 + X2, family = poisson(link = "log"), offset = log(T))
```

## Interpreting Poisson Regression Output

The intercept means that when all covariates are 0, the expected count is e to the intercept times the exposure. If T equals 1, the expected count is simply e to the intercept.

For a continuous predictor, holding other covariates fixed, a one-unit increase in that predictor multiplies the expected count by exp(coef). A positive coefficient means the expected count increases. A negative coefficient means the expected count decreases. A coefficient of 0 means no multiplicative change.

For a categorical predictor, exp(coef) compares that category with the reference category, holding other variables fixed. Never interpret a Poisson coefficient as an additive change in the count; the interpretation is multiplicative through exp(coef).

In output, `Estimate` is the coefficient on the log scale, `Std. Error` is its standard error, `z value` is estimate divided by standard error, and `Pr(>|z|)` is the p-value. A small p-value gives evidence that the predictor has a statistically significant association with the expected count. The formula left side is the outcome, and the right side contains the predictors.

## Overdispersion

The Poisson assumption is that the variance equals the mean. Overdispersion means the variance is larger than the mean. When overdispersion is present, ordinary Poisson standard errors are too small, z statistics are too large in magnitude, and p-values are too small.

A course diagnostic plot compares the fitted mean lambda with the squared residual quantity

$$
(Y_i-\lambda_i^{\mathrm{fit}})^2.
$$

If points are mostly near the line y equals x, Poisson is roughly adequate. If points are mostly above that line, overdispersion is present.

The dispersion parameter is

$$
\phi=\frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n}(y_i-\lambda_i^{\mathrm{fit}})^2/\lambda_i^{\mathrm{fit}}}{n-p-1}.
$$

If phi is close to 1, there is no strong overdispersion. If phi is greater than 1, overdispersion is present. Use quasi-Poisson or negative binomial regression when overdispersion is important.

## Quasi-Poisson Regression

Quasi-Poisson regression is used for overdispersed count data. It keeps the same mean model and the same coefficient estimates as Poisson regression, but inflates the standard errors:

$$
\mathrm{SE}_{Q}(b)=\sqrt{\phi}\,\mathrm{SE}_{P}(b).
$$

Because the standard errors become larger, the p-values usually become larger. Quasi-Poisson output reports a dispersion parameter. Its AIC is `NA`, so do not use quasi-Poisson AIC for model comparison.

In R, the family is `quasipoisson`:

```r
glm(Y ~ X1 + X2, family = quasipoisson, offset = log(T))
```

## Negative Binomial Regression

Negative binomial regression is also used for overdispersed count data. Like Poisson, the outcome takes values 0, 1, 2, and so on. Unlike Poisson, it allows variance larger than the mean. The regression uses a log link, and coefficients are interpreted multiplicatively through exp(coef), the same way as in Poisson regression.

The R function is `glm.nb()`. The output reports `theta`, a dispersion-related parameter, with its standard error. Negative binomial coefficient estimates can differ from Poisson and quasi-Poisson estimates. Negative binomial models have AIC, so AIC can be used for model comparison when comparing appropriate likelihood-based models.

## Zero-Inflated Poisson Regression

Zero-inflated Poisson regression is used when the data have more zeros than expected under a plain Poisson model. It has two sources of zeros: a structural-zero group that is always zero, and a Poisson count group that can produce zero or positive counts.

The model has a count part and a zero-inflation part:

$$
\mathrm{logit}(p)=\alpha_0+\alpha_1W_1+\cdots+\alpha_qW_q
$$

$$
\log(\lambda)=\beta_0+\beta_1X_1+\cdots+\beta_pX_p+\log(T).
$$

Here p is the probability of belonging to the structural-zero group, and lambda is the Poisson mean for the count group. The count part is interpreted as a Poisson model: exp(beta) is a multiplicative effect on the Poisson mean. The zero part is interpreted as a logistic model: exp(alpha) is an odds ratio for belonging to the structural-zero group. Do not interpret zero-part coefficients as count effects.

The mixture probabilities are

$$
Q_0=p+(1-p)P_0,\qquad Q_k=(1-p)P_k,\ k\ge 1.
$$

The ZIP mean and variance used in the slides are

$$
\mu=\lambda(1-p),\qquad \sigma^2=\lambda(1-p)(1+p\lambda).
$$

The count and zero parts may use the same predictors, different predictors, or overlapping predictors. In R, the formula has the count part before the vertical bar and the zero part after it:

```r
zeroinfl(count_formula | zero_formula, offset = log(T), dist = c("poisson"))
```

# Topic 5: Multilevel Modeling

## Clustered Data and Effective Study Size

Clustered data occur when observations in the same cluster are more similar than observations from different clusters. This violates the independence assumption used by ordinary linear, logistic, and Poisson regression. Longitudinal and repeated-measure data are clustered data because repeated observations from the same subject are dependent.

The raw sample size can be larger than the effective study size. The effective independent information is often closer to the number of independent clusters than the number of rows. If clustering is ignored, coefficient estimates may still be approximately unbiased, but standard errors are usually underestimated, test statistics become too large, p-values become too small, and Type I error increases.

## Simulation Interpretation

In the slide simulation, Dataset A has 100 independent observations. Dataset B has 20 independent observations. Dataset C copies Dataset B five times, so it has 100 rows but only the same information as Dataset B. Therefore, the coefficient estimates from B and C are the same, but treating C as 100 independent observations makes the calculated standard error too small.

Use the relationship between the standard deviation of repeated estimates and the mean standard error to judge standard error quality. If SD is approximately equal to mean SE, the SE is well estimated. If SD is greater than mean SE, the SE is underestimated and Type I error risk increases. If SD is less than mean SE, the SE is overestimated and important covariates may be missed.

## Levels and Variable Classification

Multilevel data are hierarchically clustered data. Clustered data and multilevel data are often used interchangeably, but multilevel data emphasizes hierarchy. One-level data are not multilevel data. Level 1 is the lowest observation level. Level 2 is the cluster level. Level 3 is a higher grouping level. Level identification depends on study design and domain knowledge.

In the Reading Achievement data, Level 1 is student and Level 2 is school. `school` is the cluster ID, not the covariate effect of interest. `geread` is the Level 1 outcome. `gevocab`, `gender`, `age`, and `gemath` are Level 1 variables. `senroll` and `sattend` are Level 2 variables because students in the same school share the same values.

In the cattle example, individual cattle are Level 1, herds are Level 2, and regions would be Level 3 if included. In the hospital repeated-measure example, a daily measurement is Level 1, patient is Level 2, and doctor is Level 3.

## Methods for Clustered Data

A fixed effects model handles clusters by adding cluster dummy variables. This can control observed cluster differences and give a correct conclusion for a Level 1 effect, but it has drawbacks: it cannot separately estimate Level 2 covariates fully determined by the cluster indicators, it creates many extra coefficients when there are many clusters, and its conclusion applies mainly to the observed clusters.

Robust variance estimation is also called Huber-White or sandwich variance estimation. It adjusts standard errors for clustering and is less sensitive to model assumption violations, but it mainly fixes inference for fixed effects; it does not model cluster-specific intercepts or slopes.

Survey methods are used for complex survey designs such as stratified sampling, often when estimating population quantities such as disease prevalence. Common approaches are linearisation and replication.

Use a multilevel linear model when the question asks for cluster-specific intercepts, cluster-specific slopes, Level 2 variables, or cross-level interactions. In this approach, observed clusters are treated as a random sample from a larger population of possible clusters.

## Two-Level MLM Notation

The generic Level 1 model is

$$
Y_{ij}=\beta_{0,j}+\beta_{1,j}X_{1,ij}+\cdots+\beta_{p,j}X_{p,ij}+\epsilon_{ij}.
$$

The generic Level 2 model for a cluster-specific coefficient is

$$
\beta_{r,j}=\gamma_{r,0}+\gamma_{r,1}Z_{1,j}+\cdots+\gamma_{r,q}Z_{q,j}+U_{r,j}.
$$

The index i identifies a subject or observation inside cluster j. X variables are Level 1 covariates. Z variables are Level 2 covariates. Gamma parameters are fixed effects shared at the population level. U terms are random effects, or cluster-specific deviations. The Level 1 model describes the outcome; the Level 2 model describes how cluster-specific coefficients vary across clusters.

## MLM Assumptions

The Level 1 errors are independent and normally distributed with mean 0 and variance sigma squared. The random-effect vectors are independent across clusters and follow a multivariate normal distribution with mean zero. Random effects within the same cluster may be correlated, so covariance terms are allowed. Level 1 errors are independent of the random-effect vectors.

## Random Intercept Model

A random intercept means clusters have different baselines. With no covariate, the model is

$$
Y_{ij}=\gamma_{0,0}+U_{0,j}+\epsilon_{ij}.
$$

The total variance is the random-intercept variance plus the residual variance:

$$
\mathrm{Var}(Y_{ij})=\tau_0^2+\sigma^2.
$$

A larger random-intercept variance means larger baseline differences between clusters. In `lmer()` syntax, `Y ~ 1 + (1 | cluster)` means a fixed population intercept and a cluster-specific random intercept.

For the Reading Achievement intercept-only model, the formula is `geread ~ 1 + (1 | school)`. The fixed intercept 4.30675 is the average reading achievement at the population level. The school random-intercept variance 0.3915 measures between-school baseline variation. The residual variance 5.0450 measures within-school Level 1 error variation.

## Random Intercept With Fixed Slope

A fixed slope means the predictor effect is common across clusters, while the intercept varies across clusters. The model is

$$
Y_{ij}=\gamma_{0,0}+\beta_1X_{1,ij}+U_{0,j}+\epsilon_{ij}.
$$

In `lmer()` syntax, `Y ~ 1 + X + (1 | cluster)` means a fixed intercept, a fixed slope for X, and a cluster-specific random intercept. In the Reading Achievement model `geread ~ 1 + gevocab + (1 | school)`, the fixed `gevocab` slope 0.512898 means a one-unit increase in vocabulary score increases expected `geread` by about 0.512898 at the population level, while schools have different baseline intercepts.

## Random Intercept With Random Slope

A random slope means the predictor effect varies across clusters. The model is

$$
Y_{ij}=\gamma_{0,0}+\gamma_{1,0}X_{1,ij}+U_{0,j}+U_{1,j}X_{1,ij}+\epsilon_{ij}.
$$

In `lmer()` syntax, `Y ~ 1 + X + (1 + X | cluster)` means a fixed intercept, a fixed population slope for X, a random intercept by cluster, and a random slope for X by cluster. A larger random-slope variance means larger between-cluster differences in the slope. The `Corr` column in the random effects output is the correlation between random effects, not a fixed-effect correlation.

For the Reading Achievement model `geread ~ 1 + gevocab + (1 + gevocab | school)`, the fixed intercept is about 2.00570, the fixed `gevocab` slope is about 0.52036, the random-intercept variance is about 0.2827, the random-slope variance for `gevocab` is about 0.0193, and the random intercept-slope correlation is about -0.86.

## Cross-Level Interaction

A cross-level interaction occurs when a Level 2 variable changes the effect of a Level 1 variable. If the Level 2 model for a slope is

$$
\beta_{2,j}=\gamma_{2,0}+\gamma_{2,1}Z_{1,j}+U_{2,j},
$$

then the combined model contains the interaction term

$$
Z_{1,j}X_{2,ij}.
$$

The conditional Level 1 slope is

$$
\frac{\partial E(Y_{ij})}{\partial X_{2,ij}}=\gamma_{2,0}+\gamma_{2,1}Z_{1,j}.
$$

When an interaction is present, do not interpret a main effect as a constant effect across all values of the interacting variable. In the slide example, `senroll:gevocab` means the vocabulary effect changes with school enrollment. The `gevocab` effect is 0.542388 minus 0.004612 times `senroll`.

In Homework 5 Model 3, the model includes `senroll:gevocab` and `sattend:gevocab`. The `gevocab` effect is 3.146398 minus 0.003921 times `senroll` minus 0.271146 times `sattend`. The `senroll` effect is 0.013214 minus 0.003921 times `gevocab`. The `sattend` effect is 2.663942 minus 0.271146 times `gevocab`. Because `sattend:gevocab` is negative, the `gevocab` effect decreases as `sattend` increases.

## Reading `lmer()` Output

The fixed effects table gives population-level mean changes. The intercept is the expected outcome when numeric predictors are 0 and categorical predictors are at the reference level. In the Reading data, `Female` is the reference group, so `genderMale` compares male students with female students.

The random effects table lists the grouping variable under `Groups`. `(Intercept)` variance is the random-intercept variance. A predictor variance such as `gevocab` is the random-slope variance. `Residual` variance is the within-cluster Level 1 error variance. `Std.Dev.` is the square root of the corresponding variance. `Corr` is the correlation between random effects. `Number of obs` is the number of Level 1 observations, and `groups: school` is the number of schools.

# Topic 6: Survival Analysis

## Survival Analysis, Event, and Censoring

Survival analysis is time-to-event analysis. The outcome is the length of time from the start of follow-up to the event. The event can be death, disease onset, recovery, or going out of remission. The event is often called a failure, but failure can be positive in some contexts such as recovery.

The survival time T is a random variable with T greater than or equal to 0. A lowercase t denotes a fixed time value. Censoring occurs when follow-up stops before the event occurs, so the exact event time is not observed. Censoring is not complete missingness because we still know the subject survived at least until the censoring time. Common reasons are loss to follow-up, study ending before the event, and withdrawal from the study.

The course coding is `status = 1` or `d = 1` for observed failure, and `status = 0` or `d = 0` for censoring. A plus sign in the raw survival data means the observation is censored. Censored subjects are not counted as failures, but their follow-up time still contributes information.

Survival analysis differs from linear regression because the outcome is time to event, not a continuous outcome treated without censoring. It differs from logistic regression because the outcome is not just whether an event occurred; it includes when the event occurred and handles censoring.

## Survivor Function and Hazard Function

The survivor function is

$$
S(t)=P(T>t).
$$

It gives the probability of surviving longer than time t. It starts at 1, is non-increasing, and approaches 0 as time goes to infinity. A higher survival curve usually means better survival prognosis.

The hazard function is

$$
h(t)=\lim_{\Delta t\to 0}\frac{S(t)-S(t+\Delta t)}{\Delta t S(t)}
=-\frac{d}{dt}\log S(t).
$$

The hazard is non-negative and has no upper bound. Intuitively, it is the instantaneous risk of failure around time t among those still surviving at time t. When the survival curve decreases faster, the hazard is larger.

The slides show typical hazard shapes: constant hazard for healthy people, increasing hazard for leukemia patients not responding to treatment, decreasing hazard for people recovering from surgery, and first increasing then decreasing hazard for TB patients.

The average hazard rate is

$$
\bar h=\frac{\text{total number of failures}}{\sum_i t_i}.
$$

A censored subject contributes follow-up time to the denominator but is not counted in the numerator.

## Kaplan-Meier Curve

The Kaplan-Meier curve estimates the survivor function when censoring exists. It uses ordered failure times, risk sets, failures, and censorings. The notation in the slides is: t(f) is an ordered failure time, n_f is the number at risk just before t(f), m_f is the number of failures at t(f), and q_f is the number censored in the interval from t(f) up to but not including t(f+1).

The risk set update is

$$
n_{f+1}=n_f-m_f-q_f.
$$

The Kaplan-Meier initial value and recursion are

$$
S_{\mathrm{KM}}(t_{(0)})=1
$$

$$
S_{\mathrm{KM}}(t_{(f)})=\frac{n_f-m_f}{n_f}S_{\mathrm{KM}}(t_{(f-1)}).
$$

The first step is calculated by multiplying 1 by the proportion still not failed at the first failure time. The curve is a step function: it is flat between failure times and drops only at failure times. Censoring does not make the curve drop; failures make it drop. If there is no censoring, the KM estimate equals the number surviving past the failure time divided by the original sample size.

In R `survfit()` output, `n.risk` is n_f, `n.event` is m_f, and `survival` is the Kaplan-Meier estimate.

## Log-Rank Test

The log-rank test formally compares two Kaplan-Meier survival curves overall. The null hypothesis is that the two groups have the same survival experience.

At each combined failure time, m_1f and m_2f are the observed failures in groups 1 and 2, and n_1f and n_2f are the risk sets in groups 1 and 2. Expected failures are calculated under the null hypothesis of no group difference:

$$
e_{1f}=\frac{n_{1f}}{n_{1f}+n_{2f}}(m_{1f}+m_{2f})
$$

$$
e_{2f}=\frac{n_{2f}}{n_{1f}+n_{2f}}(m_{1f}+m_{2f}).
$$

Observed minus expected compares what happened with what would be expected under equal survival. M_1 and M_2 are total observed failures. E_1 and E_2 are total expected failures. For two groups, the log-rank statistic follows a chi-squared distribution with 1 degree of freedom under the null. A small p-value means there is a significant survival difference between the groups.

## Confidence Interval for KM Curve

A confidence interval has the form estimate plus or minus critical value times standard error. The estimate is random because it is calculated from a random sample, while the true parameter is an unknown constant.

For the Kaplan-Meier curve, S_KM(t) estimates the true S(t). The 95 percent confidence interval used in the slides is

$$
S_{\mathrm{KM}}(t)\pm 1.96\sqrt{V(t)}.
$$

Greenwood's formula is used to calculate V(t). In R output, `std.err` is the standard error, `lower` is the lower confidence bound, and `upper` is the upper confidence bound. If the upper bound is greater than 1, it is set to 1.

For example, in the leukemia treatment group at time 6, survival is 0.857 and standard error is 0.0764, so the 95 percent CI is approximately 0.707 to 1 after capping the upper bound. At time 10, survival is about 0.753 and standard error is 0.0963, so the 95 percent CI is about 0.564 to 0.942. A 93 percent CI uses the same logic but a different confidence level and critical value.

## Cox Proportional Hazards Model

The Cox proportional hazards model relates survival time to covariates while handling censoring. The data format is survival time T, status d, and covariates X. The model is

$$
h(t,X_1,\ldots,X_p)=h_0(t)e^{\beta_1X_1+\cdots+\beta_pX_p}.
$$

The baseline hazard h_0(t) is the hazard when all covariates equal 0. It only needs to be non-negative; Cox PH does not require specifying its exact shape. The exponentiated linear predictor is always positive, so the hazard stays non-negative. Standard Cox PH covariates are time-independent. If covariates depend on time, use an extended Cox model.

## Hazard Ratio and Cox Output

For two covariate values X star and X, the hazard ratio is

$$
HR=e^{\beta_1(X_1^*-X_1)+\cdots+\beta_p(X_p^*-X_p)}.
$$

Under the Cox PH model, the hazard ratio is positive and does not depend on time. HR greater than 1 means higher hazard and worse survival for the numerator group or larger covariate value. HR equal to 1 means equal hazard. HR less than 1 means lower hazard and better survival.

For a one-unit increase in a continuous predictor, the adjusted HR is exp(coef). For a binary or categorical predictor, exp(coef) compares that level with the reference level. In Cox output, `coef` is the log hazard ratio, `exp(coef)` is the hazard ratio, `se(coef)` is its standard error, `z` is coefficient divided by standard error, and the p-value tests evidence for an association with survival time. `lower .95` and `upper .95` are the 95 percent confidence interval for the HR. If the HR confidence interval contains 1, the effect is not significant at the 0.05 level.

## Leukemia Cox Example

In the leukemia Cox example, survival time is weeks until going out of remission, and the event is going out of remission. The covariates are treatment group and log WBC.

The group-only Cox model is

```r
Surv(time, status) ~ Group
```

`GroupTreatment` has coefficient -1.5092 and HR 0.2211. Treatment hazard is about 22.1 percent of placebo hazard. The p-value 0.000229 is very small, so treatment has significantly lower risk than placebo.

The adjusted Cox model is

```r
Surv(time, status) ~ Group + logWBC
```

`GroupTreatment` has HR 0.2742 and p-value 0.00217, so treatment remains protective after adjusting for log WBC. `logWBC` has coefficient 1.6043, HR 4.9746, and p-value 1.11e-6. For patients in the same group, a one-unit increase in log WBC is associated with about 4.9746 times the hazard, so higher log WBC indicates worse prognosis.

## Cox PH Assumption

The Cox PH assumption is that hazard ratios are constant over time:

$$
\frac{h(t,X^*)}{h(t,X)}=\theta,
$$

where theta does not depend on t. Hazards crossing violates this assumption because the hazard ratio changes over time. In the surgery versus no surgery example, early risk is higher for surgery, but later the benefit of surgery appears; the hazard curves cross, so the HR direction changes over time and ordinary Cox PH is not appropriate.

The goodness-of-fit test or `cox.zph()` checks the PH assumption. It gives p-values for each variable and a global p-value. Large p-values mean no strong evidence against the PH assumption. Small p-values mean the PH assumption may be violated.

In the leukemia group-only model, Group p-value and GLOBAL p-value are both 0.85, so PH looks satisfied. In the model with Group and log WBC, Group p-value is 0.99, log WBC p-value is 0.92, and GLOBAL p-value is 0.99, so PH looks satisfied.

## Evans Homework Example

The Evans homework uses 50 subjects from the Evans County subset. `Time` is survival time. `Status` is 1 for event observed and 0 for censored. `Group` has levels `NoHD` and `HD`. `Occupation` has levels `Education`, `Engineering`, and `Business`. `MonthlyIncome` is measured in thousand dollars. There are 25 subjects in NoHD and 25 in HD, with 47 total events.

For the Kaplan-Meier table, NoHD survival starts at 0.96 at time 1.8 and is 0.12 after the last observed failure at time 11.7. HD survival drops faster and reaches 0 at time 11.4. This indicates the HD group has shorter survival than the NoHD group.

The Evans log-rank test uses

```r
survdiff(Surv(Time, Status) ~ Group)
```

The output gives NoHD observed failures 22 and expected failures 30.8; HD observed failures 25 and expected failures 16.2. The chi-square statistic is about 8 on 1 degree of freedom, and the p-value is about 0.0047. Since this is below 0.05, reject equal survival curves; HD has shorter survival than NoHD.

The 93 percent confidence interval for NoHD is obtained with

```r
survfit(Surv(Time, Status) ~ 1,
        data = subset(EvansData, Group == "NoHD"),
        conf.int = 0.93)
```

Examples from the homework are: at time 1.8, survival is 0.96 with CI 0.889 to 1.000; at time 9.2, survival is 0.40 with CI 0.222 to 0.578; at time 11.7, survival is 0.12 with CI 0.002 to 0.238.

The Evans Cox model is

```r
Surv(Time, Status) ~ Group + Occupation + MonthlyIncome
```

`GroupHD` has HR 1.8214 and p-value 0.0717. Holding occupation and monthly income fixed, HD has about 1.82 times the hazard of NoHD, but it is not significant at 0.05. `OccupationEducation` has HR 0.6486 and p-value 0.2486; it is not significant. `OccupationEngineering` has HR 0.8034 and p-value 0.5573; it is not significant. `MonthlyIncome` has HR 0.7682 and p-value 0.0400. Each extra thousand dollars of monthly income is associated with lower hazard, and this effect is significant at 0.05.

The overall model tests are likelihood ratio 13.04 on 4 df with p about 0.01, Wald 12.64 with p about 0.01, and score/logrank 13.03 with p about 0.01.

The Evans `cox.zph()` output gives Group p-value 0.68, Occupation p-value 0.12, MonthlyIncome p-value 0.55, and GLOBAL p-value 0.35. All are larger than 0.05, so there is no strong evidence against the Cox proportional hazards assumption.
